Artificial Intelligence in Organizations: Implications for Information Systems Research

Hind Benbya, Stella Pachidi, Sirkka Jarvenpaa • JAIS (2021)

This research agenda proposes a comprehensive framework organizing AI in organizations around four core capabilities (Automation, Engagement, Insight/Decisions, Innovation) and ten fundamental tensions—providing practitioners with a systematic approach to anticipate capability-specific challenges and adopt appropriate governance strategies.

Framework Structure

  • Automation capability: Physical robots, RPA, ML for structured tasks—navigating substitution (tasks vs jobs) and augmentation (automation vs human judgment) tensions
  • Engagement capability: Chatbots, conversational AI, emotion AI—balancing humanlike vs machinelike interaction and managing emotion AI privacy/ethics concerns
  • Insight/Decisions capability: ML algorithms, neural networks, predictive analytics—addressing accountability gaps, human vs machine bias, rationality vs judgment, and learning vs myopia
  • Innovation capability: Generative AI, deep learning for R&D—managing exploration vs exploitation trade-offs and credit allocation for AI-generated innovations

Key Tensions

  • Substitution of tasks not jobs: Robots rising from 10% to 25% by 2025 (Sirkin), but most occupations have replaceable tasks without full replaceability (Brynjolfsson)—manage power reconfigurations as some groups deskilled while others elevated
  • Automation vs augmentation: Binary choice between efficiency (automation) and judgment (augmentation)—overemphasis on either creates detrimental performance consequences; requires hybrid approaches
  • Humanlike vs machinelike: Anthropomorphism increases conversions but triggers oscillation effect where users alternate between treating bot as human and probing limits—context determines appropriate level
  • Emotion AI ethics: Sentiment/facial/voice monitoring enhances adaptation but raises privacy, nudging abuse, and cultural bias concerns—consent and boundaries required
  • Accountability gaps: Machines lack intentionality required for responsibility; humans can't take responsibility when algorithmic decisions made at superhuman speed/scale—explainability and protocols needed
  • Human vs machine bias: Automated decisions assumed bias-free, but training data creates systematic discrimination (racist recidivism predictions, gender-biased hiring)—requires audits and transparency
  • Rationality vs judgment: ML overcomes bounded rationality but overreliance erodes intuitive judgment, creates emotional detachment, and causes passive acceptance—maintain human involvement
  • Learning vs myopia: Path dependency and temporal myopia from training on historical data—unprecedented situations (COVID-19) require human oversight when historical patterns insufficient
  • Exploration vs exploitation: Large training data favors exploitation (existing knowledge) over exploration (novelty)—abundant data enables AI innovation, limited data requires human creativity
  • Credit allocation: Algorithms increasingly autonomous innovators, but authorship unclear—proposed solution grants credit to programmers/owners, raising incentive allocation questions
