The Cybernetic Teammate: A Field Experiment on Generative AI Reshaping Teamwork and Expertise

Dell'Acqua, Ayoubi, Lifshitz-Assaf, Sadun, Mollick, Mollick, Han, Goldman, Nair, Taub, Lakhani • HBS Working Paper 25-043 (2025)

Field experiment with 776 Procter & Gamble professionals reveals GenAI can act as a "cybernetic teammate" that replicates three pillars of human teamwork: performance gains, expertise sharing, and social engagement. Individuals with AI matched the quality of two-person cross-functional teams without AI, broke down functional silos between Commercial and R&D roles, and reported emotional benefits matching human collaboration—fundamentally reshaping optimal team size and structure for knowledge work.

Performance: AI Enables Individuals to Match Teams

  • Quality improvements over solo baseline: Teams without AI +0.24 SD (p < 0.05), individuals with AI +0.37 SD (p < 0.01), teams with AI +0.39 SD (p < 0.01)—AI-enabled individuals matched traditional team performance
  • Breakthrough outcomes: Teams with AI 3× more likely to produce top 10% solutions (9.2 percentage point increase from 5.8% baseline)—AI complements teamwork for exceptional results
  • Efficiency gains: Individuals with AI completed tasks 16.4% faster while producing significantly longer, more comprehensive solutions—double benefit of speed and thoroughness
  • Confidence gap: Despite higher objective quality, AI users were 9.2 percentage points less likely to expect top 10% placement—workers underestimate AI's contribution

Expertise: Breaking Down Functional Silos

  • Boundary-spanning knowledge: Without AI, Commercial professionals proposed commercial solutions, R&D proposed technical solutions. With AI, both produced similarly balanced technical-commercial solutions regardless of background—replicating team-level knowledge integration
  • Democratizing task expertise: Non-core-job employees with AI matched teams containing core-job experts—AI substituted for experience and guidance typically provided by veteran teammates
  • Reduced dominance effects: Teams without AI showed bimodal distributions (clustering around dominant member's perspective). Teams with AI produced uniform distributions—both perspectives contributed more equally
  • Quality independence: Solution quality didn't vary by technical orientation—AI-enabled boundary spanning maintained effectiveness across the technical-commercial spectrum

Sociality: Positive Emotional Responses

  • Positive emotions: Individuals with AI +0.457 SD (p < 0.01), teams with AI +0.635 SD (p < 0.01)—significantly higher excitement, energy, and enthusiasm compared to baseline
  • Negative emotions: Both individuals and teams with AI showed -0.23 SD reduction (p < 0.05) in anxiety, frustration, and distress
  • Matching human collaboration: Individuals with AI reported emotional responses matching or exceeding teams without AI—AI substitutes for some motivational benefits of human teamwork
  • Adoption correlation: Participants reporting larger increases in expected future AI use showed more positive emotions—positive experiences may drive adoption

Organizational Implications

  • Rethinking optimal team size: AI-enabled individuals can handle tasks that previously required cross-functional teams—opportunities for more flexible, efficient structures while reducing coordination costs
  • Efficiency vs. breakthrough trade-off: Use individual+AI for average quality with speed; use team+AI for top-tier outcomes. Different optimization strategies for different business needs
  • AI prompting as critical skill: Participants were relatively inexperienced, suggesting benefits may be lower bound. Training in boundary-spanning AI interaction (using AI to reason across functional domains) becomes strategic priority
  • Balancing learning objectives: While AI enables smaller teams and faster work, traditional teamwork serves mentorship and culture-building functions—explicit policies needed for when these secondary benefits justify maintaining larger structures

The Cybernetic Teammate: A Field Experiment on Generative AI Reshaping Teamwork and Expertise. Harvard Business School Working Paper No. 25-043.

Experimental design: 776 P&G professionals (Commercial and R&D functions) randomized to 2×2 conditions (individual/team × with/without GPT-4) in one-day virtual product innovation workshop. Teams comprised 1 Commercial + 1 R&D professional. Solutions evaluated by blind human experts on quality (1–10 scale). Participants reported emotions before and after task.

Note: "Cybernetic" references Norbert Wiener's concept of feedback-regulated systems that interact dynamically with their environment—here, AI functioning as an active collaborator providing real-time responses, bridging expertise gaps, and influencing emotional states, rather than a passive tool.
