Conversational AI increases political knowledge as effectively as self-directed internet search

Lennart Luettgau, Hannah Kirk, Kobi Hackenburg, Christopher Summerfield et al. • arXiv:2509.05219 (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

The authors combine a nationally representative UK survey with randomized trials that pit GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, and Mistral chatbots against traditional search. They find that voters already lean on AI for election research, that guardrailed models lift belief in true facts and suppress misinformation as effectively as search, and that even persuasive or sycophantic prompts fail to shift attitudes. GPT-4o delivered the quickest task completions, Claude 3.5 matched its epistemic gains, and Mistral trailed slightly while staying within overlapping confidence intervals.

🧭

Executive quick take

Chatbots are already a mainstream election research tool: roughly one in eight UK voters turned to them in 2024. In controlled trials they matched web search at boosting true beliefs, did not amplify misinformation, and stayed neutral even when pushed toward persuasion, suggesting that current guardrails hold up under everyday stress.

  • Adoption reality: 32% of chatbot users applied them to political decisions during the UK general election campaign.
  • Epistemic parity: Conversational AI lifted agreement with true facts by ~0.7 Likert points and nudged misinformation down just like search.
  • Prompt resilience: Persuasive and sycophantic system prompts delivered statistically negligible attitude shifts.

💼 Business relevance

Treat guardrailed chatbots as a first-class electoral research channel. These results show they already serve mainstream voters without the misinformation spike many risk teams fear.

  • News publishers: Editorial leaders can plan subscription messaging that complements verified reporting rather than compete with it.
  • Platforms & ad networks: Trust and safety teams can recalibrate misinformation risk playbooks knowing persuasive prompts did not amplify false claims.
  • Consumer brands: Customer-experience leaders can justify guided assistants for governance updates because chatbots delivered faster answers without eroding trust metrics.
  • Policy teams: Regulator liaison groups can point to comparable search-sized epistemic gains when drafting disclosures or mitigation plans.

Model-by-model signal

  • GPT-4o: Highest true-belief lift and the fastest completions; search parity sits well within its 95% confidence interval.
  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet: Near-identical epistemic gains with slightly longer dialogues, reinforcing that prompt style matters more than model choice.
  • Mistral Large: Marginally slower and a touch less confident, yet still delivers positive true-minus-false deltas when nudged with the paper's guardrail prompts.

Usage snapshot

Surveyed UK voters leaned on AI for the same tasks they once reserved for search.

  • 9% of adults used chatbots for political info in the four weeks around polling day.
  • Chatbot users ranked politics alongside work and education as top scenarios.
  • Younger voters (18–34) and degree-holders were roughly twice as likely to adopt; over-55 usage stayed in single digits.
  • Top intents: checking party manifestos, fact-checking debate quotes, and comparing policy calculators.

Epistemic impact

The trials emphasise belief change, not just anecdotal answer quality.

  • True statements gained ~0.7 Likert points post-research across both channels.
  • Misinformation belief dropped similarly, confirming symmetrical guardrails.
  • No measurable drift in trust, private beliefs, or extremism indices, even under persuasive prompts.

Terminology

  • Conversational AI condition: Guided sessions with GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, or Mistral using updated guardrails.
  • Internet search control: Participants free-roamed via search engines to research the same prompts.
  • Persuasive/sycophantic prompts: System messages that force agreement with a random or user-held stance.
  • Epistemic health measures: Likert-scale agreement with curated true and false statements, plus trust, belief, and extremism composites.

🧪 Experiments & evidence

  • Post-election survey: Weighted UK sample (N=2,499) detailing chatbot adoption, perceived accuracy, usefulness, and bias.
  • Four-issue RCTs: 2,858 participants researched climate, immigration, criminal justice, and COVID policy via AI or search.
  • Prompt interventions: Persuasive and sycophantic system prompts showed no significant divergence from neutral GPT-4o, with overlapping 95% confidence intervals.
  • Latency readout: Conversational AI shortened task completion time by ~2 minutes versus search.
  • Guardrail compliance: Over 60% of deliberate stress prompts triggered neutralising behaviour or refusals, matching search in outcome quality.

Scope & limitations

  • Findings cover the 2024 UK general election; other electorates or multi-party systems may surface different behaviours.
  • Only guardrailed frontier APIs were tested; open-weight or domain-tuned models could drift without equivalent alignment layers.
  • Trials measured single-session research; long-run attitude shifts, coordinated persuasion, or cross-platform journeys remain untested.

🔭 Forward-looking roadmap

  • Replicate in other electorates and longer campaign windows to watch for drift.
  • Instrument production chatbots to detect when prompts exceed tested guardrail regimes.
  • Blend conversational and retrieval experiences to preserve guardrails while exposing primary sources.
  • Develop monitoring for persuasion attempts that combine multiple tool calls or external links.
