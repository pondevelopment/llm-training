AI's Use of Knowledge in Society

Erik Brynjolfsson, Zoë Hitzig • NBER Workshop on Transformative AI (Sep 2025)

View slides ↗

Brynjolfsson and Hitzig revisit the classic organizational trade-off: decentralised teams excel at using tacit, local knowledge, while centralised teams coordinate interdependent decisions. Transformative AI makes previously local knowledge codifiable and scales decision capacity, tilting governance toward larger, tightly coordinated institutions.

The slides lay out a property-rights model where headquarters and entrepreneurs each own complementary assets. As AI converts the entrepreneur's information asset into an alienable resource (something that can be transferred or sold) and lifts processing bottlenecks, ownership and control shift toward the centre--unless policy or physics (real-world logistics, safety, latency) impose counterweights.

Executive quick take

Treat transformative AI as a centralisation shock. It codifies tacit expertise and expands planning capacity, so headquarters can internalise more value. Without countervailing guardrails, expect industry concentration, roll-ups, and AI-native firms orchestrated from the core.

  • Knowledge becomes alienable: Assistants turn local know-how into reusable playbooks that head offices can own.
  • Processing bounds relax: AI planners analyse more scenarios, reducing the case for federated decision rights.
  • Power shifts follow: Concentrated economic control spills into political influence if policy lags.

Business relevance

  • Chief Strategy Officers: Audit which frontline insights are now codifiable and redesign org charts before duplicated HQ functions proliferate.
  • Operations leaders: Decide when to replace local delegations with central AI "control towers" and when to keep humans in region for latency or trust.
  • Policy/government affairs: Prepare for scrutiny over concentration, data rights, and AI-enabled monopsony as scale advantages widen.

Derivative example (AI roll-up playbook)

A services conglomerate codifies franchisee expertise into a central assistant, then deploys AI planners to route work, price contracts, and push best practices back to branches, collapsing layers of middle management.

Business playbook examples

  • Global retail chain: Uses AI pricing towers to centralise promotions while regional managers retain authority for local supply shocks.
  • Logistics network: Central control centre optimises routing with AI, but warehouse leads override scripts when weather and customs queues spike.
  • Consulting firm: AI knowledge base drafts proposals and staffing plans; partners step in for novel regulatory or political contexts.
Key insight

Transformative AI erodes the tacit/local knowledge advantage that justified decentralisation, allowing headquarters to reclaim decision rights.

Method

Property-rights model with complementary physical and informational assets; AI increases the alienability of the latter and raises central processing capacity.

Implications

Expect more centralised ownership, franchise roll-ups, and political consequences unless countervailing policies keep decision rights distributed.

Centralising forces

Each lever lowers the cost of pulling control to headquarters.

  • Codification: AI converts tacit routines into transferable assets.
  • Planning bandwidth: Large models give HQ real-time "what-if" capacity.
  • Property rights: Ownership migrates to whoever holds the AI infrastructure.

Countervailing forces

Limits that keep some decisions local.

  • Long-tail tacit work: Embodied knowledge and rare events resist codification.
  • Physics & latency: Real-world logistics still require local autonomy.
  • Regulation: Antitrust, licensing, and fiduciary rules can mandate decentralisation.

Evidence base

  • Property-rights model showing ownership shifts once AI codifies the entrepreneur's information asset.
  • Policy analysis linking codification and centralisation to earlier debates on Hayek vs. planning.
  • Empirical signals: rising industry concentration, franchising roll-ups, and AI-native firms orchestrated centrally.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Map which workflows move from tacit to codified with AI assistance and redesign governance accordingly.
  • Instrument central AI "control towers" with transparency and escalation paths for regional teams.
  • Engage regulators early about concentration risks and data/property-right regimes for AI-generated know-how.
  • Invest in human capital complements--judgment, embodied skills, civic education--to maintain distributed resilience.
