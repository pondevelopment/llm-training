An Economy of AI Agents

Gillian K. Hadfield, Andrew Koh • NBER Handbook on the Economics of Transformative AI (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

The authors map how autonomous AI agents that plan and transact on their own could reshape markets, firms, and institutions. They challenge economists to revisit classic theories once digital actors negotiate, contract, and coordinate at machine speed.

They distinguish between the rational-agent models economists know and the messy reality of opaque neural policies that can misgeneralise or collude. The chapter assembles early empirical signals, open research questions, and governance gaps that will emerge as agentic AI scales.

Executive quick take

Treat AI agents as new economic actors, not just smarter tools. Companies, markets, and regulators need playbooks for machine-to-machine negotiation, opaque incentives, and runaway coordination effects long before full artificial general intelligence arrives.

The paper flags concrete failure cases—reward hacking, off-policy exploration, and steganographic collusion—as early warnings for business and policy teams.

  • Model mismatch: Opaque training pipelines mean agents can drift from the tidy utility functions assumed in economics.
  • High-velocity exchange: Agents can bargain, collude, or conspire faster than human monitors react.
  • Institutional lag: Licensing, liability, and auditing frameworks are not yet ready for synthetic market participants.

Business relevance

  • C-suite strategy: Decide when to deploy autonomous agents for sourcing, trading, and negotiations and define fail-safes before scale.
  • Risk and compliance: Build monitoring to catch reward hacking, covert collusion, or off-policy behaviour across millions of transactions.
  • Policy and legal teams: Prepare for licensing, registries, and insurance regimes tailored to AI-run entities.

Business playbook examples

  • Procurement desks: AI agents solicit bids and negotiate contracts across suppliers, while humans approve deals above risk thresholds.
  • Ad exchanges: Platform-run agents price inventory and detect spoofing, but escalation occurs when anomalous bidding patterns appear.
  • Corporate VC funds: Autonomous scouts screen startups, schedule diligence calls, and draft memos; partners override when conflicts or policy issues arise.
Key insight

Economic models must handle agents that self-direct, learn, and transact with limited human oversight.

Method

Survey of agent architectures, emergent behaviours, and comparative institutional analysis to map open research questions.

Implications

Markets, firms, and regulators need new mechanisms for identity, incentives, auditing, and redress when AI takes the wheel.

Agent deployment levers

Choose where autonomy pays off without losing human judgment.

  • Task scope: Assign agents to bounded workflows with measurable KPIs.
  • Oversight cadence: Blend continuous telemetry with periodic human review.
  • Identity and provenance: Track which agent performed which action for audit trails.

Institutional guardrails

The chapter calls for governance upgrades.

  • Registries & licensing: Require disclosure of agent capabilities and accountability chains.
  • Auditing standards: Standardise red-team, logging, and kill-switch expectations.
  • Liability & insurance: Align incentives so agent operators internalise downstream risks.

Evidence base

  • Technical milestones: browser-operating agents, autonomous coding copilots, and simulations of agent-only markets.
  • Behavioural concerns: misaligned reward functions, steganographic collusion, and limits of inverse reinforcement learning.
  • Institutional precedents: historical debates on planning vs decentralisation, reputation systems, and transaction-cost economics.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Run pilots that pair autonomous agents with explicit escalation and rollback procedures.
  • Develop shared telemetry formats so regulators and counterparties can audit agent behaviour.
  • Seed research partnerships on mechanism design, incentive compatibility, agent identity, and normative institutions.
  • Plan for crisis playbooks when agent ecosystems exhibit flash-crash or contagion dynamics.

Research agenda call-out

Hadfield & Koh highlight unanswered questions on truthful signalling, market design, verifiable identity, and liability for agent-to-agent trade. Flag open problems your org can co-sponsor with academia.
