AI Exposure and the Adaptive Capacity of American Workers

Sam Manning, Tomás Aguirre • GovAI & Foundation for American Innovation (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

Manning and Aguirre pair AI exposure data with an adaptive capacity index that measures how well workers can navigate job transitions. Instead of viewing exposure as inevitable displacement, they show where the workforce is resilient and where pockets of vulnerability cluster.

Their index blends four ingredients—liquid savings, transferable skills, local job density, and age—for 300 occupations covering 91.9% of U.S. employment. The result helps policymakers triage support, distinguishing exposed workers likely to bounce back from those who may need targeted interventions.

Executive quick take

AI exposure alone is a blunt instrument. Cross it with adaptive capacity to spot both resilience and risk. Administrative and clerical workers surface as the most exposed with the fewest cushions, while many professional jobs combine high exposure with strong fallback options.

  • Two-sided dashboard: Exposure shows where tasks may change; adaptive capacity reveals whether workers can absorb the shock.
  • 7.2M at risk: About 5.3% of the workforce is both highly exposed and low capacity—mostly female, clerical roles.
  • Policy triage: Data-driven targeting beats blanket programs when millions can adapt and millions cannot.

Business relevance

  • Chief People Officers: Map your workforce against exposure and adaptive capacity to prioritise reskilling budgets.
  • Learning & development teams: Build rapid upskilling tracks for clerical and support roles where capacity lags.
  • Workforce planners: Stress test location strategy—thin job markets compound low capacity when AI retools roles.

Business playbook examples

  • Bank operations: Pair automation pilots with mobility stipends and cross-training into compliance roles.
  • Healthcare systems: Reinvent medical admin pathways into patient logistics and AI-augmented coding jobs.
  • Retail headquarters: Offer finance and analytics bootcamps to administrative assistants flagged as high exposure.
Key insight

Exposure ≠ inevitable unemployment—the buffer from savings, skills, geography, and age matters.

Method

Combine O*NET, ACS, CPS, SCF, and CBP data into employment-weighted z-scores merged with task-level AI exposure estimates.

Implications

Invest in targeted safety nets, retraining, and mobility supports where exposure collides with low adaptive capacity.

Adaptive capacity levers

Each component points to a policy or HR lever.

  • Liquid savings: Emergency funds, wage insurance, or targeted subsidies.
  • Skill transferability: Microcredentialing and modular training to diversify skill portfolios.
  • Job density: Remote work, relocation support, or employer partnerships in thin markets.
  • Age: Tailor support for mid-career vs near-retirement workers.

Signals for policymakers

The dataset highlights where to aim scarce resources.

  • Clerical hotspots: Secretaries, administrative assistants, and office clerks dominate the high-risk cluster.
  • Gender skew: Seventy-five percent of the high-risk group is female?design supports that account for caregiving burdens.
  • Measurement playbook: The z-score approach is transparent and reproducible for other regions.

Evidence base

  • 300-occupation map covering 91.9% of employment with exposure and adaptive capacity percentiles.
  • Finds 21.5M workers both highly exposed and high-capacity; 7.2M workers exposed with low capacity.
  • Robustness checks across alternative data sources, weighting schemes, and routine-task measures.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Publish open adaptive-capacity dashboards that combine firm data with regional labour stats.
  • Co-fund rapid credential programs that move clerical staff into higher-capacity roles.
  • Expand wage insurance and portable benefits pilots in low-capacity, high-exposure occupations.
  • Track demographic equity by pairing adaptive measures with gender, race, and age slices.
