Artificial Intelligence, Competition, and Welfare

Susan Athey, Fiona Scott Morton • NBER Transformative AI Conference (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

Athey and Scott Morton treat upstream AI providers as concentrated suppliers that can tax adoption twice: a usage toll on every inference and an access charge on every firm. Across three general-equilibrium models they separate technical efficiency from market-power leakage and show how both levers can leave unskilled workers worse off even when AI lowers production costs.

Plain-language explainer

Picture AI vendors as toll operators. You pay an entrance tax to join the highway (access) and a charge at every gantry (usage). If only a few firms own the tollbooths, savings from faster trips leak overseas instead of funding local wages or product variety. The models reveal which policies plug those leaks and when the tolls collapse demand for labor.

The paper's headline warning is the "double harm" zone: when AI is cheap, displaced workers struggle to reabsorb; when markups rise, firms pass costs on and variety shrinks. The authors derive an adoption frontier that ties feasible price combinations to workers' outside options, so capping only one fee just shifts rents to the other.

🧭

Executive quick take

Market power in the AI stack is now a macro variable. Usage fees raise unit costs, access fees ration entry, and both can leak income abroad. Competition pressure and labour absorption policies must move together or real wages and welfare fall even as the technology becomes more efficient.

  • Model sequence: Traded-goods baseline, non-traded CPI channel, and a discrete-adoption frontier each add a new failure mode.
  • Double harm: Cheaper AI can still cut unskilled wages; higher prices pile on via CPI spikes and lost variety.
  • Policy linkage: Dual-fee regulation plus credible labour-market supports are the only way to keep rents onshore.

💼 Business relevance

  • Chief economists: Model AI scenarios with endogenous CPI and wage responses, not just cost curves.
  • Procurement leads: Stress-test vendor offers against the adoption frontier — ensure access fees do not choke partner variety.
  • Strategy and ops: Fund labour transitions in advance; downstream productivity gains only materialise if displaced staff land in high-demand services.

Business playbook examples

  • Search & ads platforms: Blend in-house models with open or allied suppliers to keep both usage and access fees contestable.
  • Contact centres: Link AI rollout gates to reemployment velocity metrics so substitution shocks stay politically viable.
  • Cloud providers: Publish transparent fee ladders and bundle labour-support services to align with coming regulation.

Key insight

Technical efficiency is not enough. Income leakage, CPI pressure, and entry rationing can erase AI productivity gains.

Method

Three open-economy GE models: traded baseline, non-traded closure with endogenous CPI, and a CES/free-entry adoption frontier with two-part tariffs.

Implications

Cap both usage and access fees, keep domestic AI supply competitive, and build labour absorption instruments before shocks hit.

Model 1 — Traded baseline

  • Stolper-Samuelson logic: cheaper AI shifts wages depending on sectoral skill intensity.
  • Double-harm emerges when AI substitutes strongly for unskilled labour yet markups later rise.

Model 2 — Non-traded CPI channel

  • Introducing a non-traded sector makes CPI endogenous; AI expansion can spike local prices.
  • Real wages can fall even if nominal wages rise because households rebalance toward pricier non-tradables.

Model 3 — Discrete adoption frontier

  • AI adoption is all-or-nothing and removes unskilled labour from costs.
  • Two-part tariffs distort both unit costs (usage) and variety (access); monopolists price along the adoption frontier.

📊 Adoption frontier mechanics

Feasible fee pairs lie on a downward-sloping frontier pX ≤ pX,max(φ). When rivals adopt, displaced workers' outside options improve, so sustaining adoption requires either lower usage prices or lower access fees.

  • Access fees leak income abroad and ration entry, shrinking product variety.
  • Usage fees raise marginal cost, pushing CPI up when non-tradables dominate consumption.
  • Binding frontier means capping one fee simply shifts the monopolist onto the other lever unless both are disciplined.

📊 Evidence base

  • Analytical GE results showing wage sign reversals as AI prices move and skill intensity flips.
  • Non-traded closure proofs where CPI effects create simultaneous real wage losses for all workers.
  • Adoption frontier derivations with conditions for monopolists pricing on the boundary.
  • Comparative statics demonstrating how access fees cut entry and variety, amplifying welfare losses.

🔮 Forward-looking roadmap

  • Audit both usage and access fee clauses; publish adoption-frontier style dashboards for large AI contracts.
  • Invest in labour absorption capacity (education, health, care, public services) before displacement spikes.
  • Coordinate industrial policy so domestic AI capability and open models keep upstream markups in check.
  • Model fiscal tools — from wage insurance to UBI — alongside competition policy to fund societal buffers.
