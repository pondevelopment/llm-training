Artificial Intelligence, Competition, and Welfare

Susan Athey, Fiona Scott Morton • NBER Transformative AI Conference (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

Athey and Scott Morton treat upstream AI providers as a concentrated industry that sells usage and access rights. They show how markups on both levers can flip a technically efficient AI upgrade into falling wages, variety loss, and welfare leakage abroad.

Across open-economy general equilibrium models, they separate the productivity boost of AI from the distributional hit of market power: unskilled workers can be hurt by both cheap and expensive AI, and monopolists price on an adoption frontier unless regulators cap both fees.

Plain-language explainer

Think of AI vendors as toll operators. You pay once to join the road (access fee) and again for every trip (usage fee). When a few companies control the tollbooths, they can keep prices high, and the time you save by using the highway might not outweigh the cost. This paper measures how those tolls shift who gains from AI and which policies keep the savings in workers’ paycheques.

Executive quick take

AI’s upside is throttled by upstream market power. Usage fees raise unit costs, access fees shrink variety, and rents leak abroad. Competition policy and labour-market supports must work together or real wages can fall even as productivity rises.

  • Double harm: Unskilled workers lose when AI is cheap (substitution) and when monopolists hike prices.
  • Two pricing levers: Usage (per query/inference) and access (seat) fees both matter; capping one shifts extraction to the other.
  • Welfare leakage: Monopoly rents flow to upstream providers, so adoption doesn’t guarantee national gains.

Business relevance

  • Chief economists: Model AI adoption with realistic supplier markups to avoid over-promising productivity gains.
  • Procurement leads: Negotiate usage caps, elasticity clauses, and exit ramps when locking into foundation model vendors.
  • Policy teams: Align antitrust advocacy with labour cushions; welfare depends on both.

Business playbook examples

  • Search & ads platforms: Blend in-house models with multi-sourcing to dilute upstream bargaining power.
  • Contact centres: Pair AI assistant deployment with wage insurance and redeployment pathways for displaced agents.
  • Cloud providers: Unbundle access and usage pricing to pre-empt regulatory action and build trust with enterprise customers.
Key insight

AI market power—not just technical capability—determines whether real wages and welfare rise or fall.

Method

Open-economy GE models with traded/non-traded sectors, CES demand, discrete adoption, and monopolistic upstream pricing.

Implications

Regulate both access and usage fees, foster competition, and invest in labour absorption to keep AI gains onshore.

Policy levers

Each instrument targets a different choke point.

  • Usage fee scrutiny: Treat per-inference pricing like a commodity input—monitor margins, mandate discounts.
  • Access fee oversight: Prevent bundling and seat taxes from throttling variety.
  • Labour cushions: Wage insurance, reskilling, and mobility supports keep adoption politically viable.
  • International bargaining: Coordinate with allies to limit rent leakage to dominant suppliers.

Watchpoints for firms

Adoption strategy under market power.

  • Double-harm scenarios: Stress test unskilled wages under both falling and rising AI prices.
  • Contract design: Build clauses that scale prices with proven productivity, not seat counts.
  • Variety loss: Encourage alternative suppliers or open-source to avoid being boxed into a single stack.

Evidence base

  • General equilibrium models showing when AI lowers real wages despite efficiency gains.
  • Adoption frontier tying feasible fees to displaced workers’ outside options.
  • Comparative statics for policy caps on usage versus access pricing.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Audit AI contracts for dual-fee extraction and publish transparency dashboards.
  • Advocate multilateral competition standards for AI access/usage pricing.
  • Pair adoption with sectoral upskilling funds and rapid reemployment programmes.
  • Model rent leakage in trade policy to negotiate fairer access to AI infrastructure.
