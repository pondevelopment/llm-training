Artificial Intelligence in Research and Development

Benjamin F. Jones • NBER Transformative AI Conference (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

Jones builds a flexible model of the “ideas production function,” placing both people and AI-powered machines inside R&D. The payoff from smarter machines depends on three levers: how many research tasks AI can take on, how well it performs each task, and which complementary bottlenecks (labs, data, regulation) still bind when machine intelligence soars.

The framework spans everything from modest copilots to superintelligent lab partners and covers R&D goals ranging from drug discovery to national security. It clarifies what to measure today—task coverage, relative productivity, and bottlenecks—to forecast whether AI actually speeds up progress.

Plain-language explainer

Imagine a research shop that relies on both scientists and lab machines. Smarter AI is like upgrading the machines so they can run more experiments, interpret data faster, or even write code. The gains only stick if other ingredients—physical labs, datasets, approvals—keep pace. Jones’ model shows how to spot the new bottleneck when “intelligence” is no longer scarce.

Executive quick take

Treat AI as research capital. Track task coverage, task-specific productivity, and complementary bottlenecks to decide where machine intelligence accelerates R&D—and where it stalls.

  • Three diagnostics: What share of tasks can AI do? How good is it? Which other inputs become limiting?
  • Superhuman doesn’t mean unlimited: If experiments, data, or regulation are bottlenecks, even brilliant AI stalls.
  • Measurement agenda: Empirical tracking of coverage, productivity ratios, and bottlenecks is essential to forecast impact.

Business relevance

Operational friction

Shifting tasks to AI often bumps into physical labs, compute budgets, data pipelines, and regulatory reviews. Map these complements up front so the bottleneck moves when you add smarter machines.

  • R&D leaders: Map tasks across discovery pipelines and decide where AI has enough coverage to move the needle.
  • Chief scientists: Quantify machine vs human productivity to prioritise compute, data pipelines, or human hiring.
  • Strategy teams: Plan for new bottlenecks—lab throughput, safety reviews, compute budgets—as AI scales.

Business playbook examples

  • Pharma: Use AI to screen molecules but invest in automation for wet-lab validation to avoid new choke points.
  • Chip design: Combine AI code generation with expanded simulation farms to keep design loops tight.
  • Enterprise software: Deploy AI copilots for coding while upgrading QA and release pipelines so productivity sticks.
Key insight

AI becomes transformative when it covers many research tasks, performs them well, and complementary bottlenecks are relieved.

Method

General R&D production function with people and machine capital, allowing flexible task coverage, heterogeneous productivity, and multiple bottlenecks.

Implications

Track when intelligence stops being scarce; shift investment toward lab capacity, data rights, or regulation when they become the limiter.

Measurement priorities

The paper points to empirical work R&D teams can start now.

  • Task mapping: Inventory tasks across idea funnels; label which are machine-capable today and tomorrow.
  • Performance ratios: Benchmark AI vs human speed, quality, and cost by task.
  • Bottleneck indicators: Track lab throughput, data availability, and review cycles as AI scales.

Policy signals

Guidance for governments and funders.

  • Complementary capital: Expand lab automation, compute, and data access so AI gains are realised.
  • Regulatory agility: Streamline approvals and safety frameworks to keep innovation loops fast.
  • Workforce planning: Redirect human talent into tasks where judgement, experiment design, or stewardship remain scarce.

Evidence base

  • Closed-form models showing how task coverage, productivity ratios, and bottlenecks determine AI-driven research acceleration.
  • Comparative statics for modest vs superhuman AI and when machine intelligence substitutes or complements researchers.
  • Measurement agenda linking theory to data on task portfolios, performance metrics, and complementary capital.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Publish task-coverage dashboards that track AI vs human roles across R&D pipelines.
  • Invest in automation and data infrastructure that relieve emerging bottlenecks.
  • Run pilot projects that integrate AI with lab robotics and evaluate end-to-end throughput.
  • Collaborate with policymakers on compute access, data sharing, and safety norms for AI-driven discovery.
