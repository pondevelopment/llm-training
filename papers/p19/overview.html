Making AI Count: The Next Measurement Frontier

Diane Coyle • John Lourenze Poquiz • NBER Transformative AI Conference (Aug 2025)

View paper ↗

Coyle and Poquiz argue that national accounts (the official GDP and productivity dashboards governments publish) are set up to miss the early macro impact of generative AI. Free products, embedded AI services, and rapid quality gains all stay invisible, so GDP, productivity, and capital statistics will lag the structural shift even as behaviours change.

The chapter walks the entire AI value chain—compute, data, labour, environmental costs, outputs, process redesign, and time use—and shows where classifications, valuation rules, and data pipelines fail. Their agenda: build satellite accounts, richer task data, and private-public data-sharing before AI acceleration makes policy blind.

Plain-language explainer

Think of today’s statistics as a blurry dashboard: we see the car’s fuel purchases (cloud bills) but not how many rides the AI chauffeur gives away for free, nor how much faster passengers arrive. The authors map what new gauges governments need to spot AI-driven growth, inequality, and environmental load in real time.

Executive quick take

Plan for at least a decade where GDP, productivity, and even carbon accounts under-report AI reality. Build parallel measurement systems that watch compute, data, and time savings directly.

  • Shadow ledgers: Track AI diffusion via supplier invoices, platform telemetry, and private usage data before official stats catch up.
  • Quality-first deflators: Pair revenue series with outcome metrics so AI-driven service upgrades show up in real output.
  • Environmental twins: Bind compute expansion to power and water accounts; emissions live where the datacenter sits, not the user.
  • Task census: Monitor time reallocation and labour share fallouts as AI swaps labour for capital inside existing industries.

Business relevance

Derivative example: measurement war-room

A global services firm spins up an AI measurement squad that fuses finance data (GPT spend, cloud GPU hours), ops telemetry (workflow cycle times), and HR task logs. They mirror upcoming government classifications so policy incentives and tax credits land in their favour.

  • CFOs: Flag AI outlays embedded in SaaS bundles, capitalise own AI builds, and publish compute-adjusted productivity to investors.
  • COOs: Treat time saved by copilots as a shadow asset; redeploy it and evidence the gains for regulators or partners.
  • Sustainability leads: Align energy and water disclosures with datacentre sourcing before mandatory reporting expands scope.
  • People teams: Capture task mixes and automation exposure to anticipate labour share impacts on comp and bargaining.

AI supply-chain ledger

The chapter insists on tracking inputs from chips to talent.

  • Hardware & energy: Link GPU imports, colocated data centres, and power draw to AI production satellites.
  • Data capital: Value training corpora via cost-based ledgers, but annotate gaps where market prices emerge.
  • Talent microdata: Merge visas, LinkedIn skills, and PhD output to monitor capability bottlenecks.

Output & welfare gauges

Outputs need quality-sensitive metrics.

  • Service quality: Pair revenue with outcomes (diagnostic accuracy, SLA adherence).
  • Personalised prices: Capture AI-driven price dispersion so inflation measures stay credible.
  • Time use: Promote continuous time diaries and platform telemetry as welfare proxies.
Key insight

Standard GDP, productivity, and capital stocks will understate AI-driven change because they omit free services, embedded quality gains, and task reorganisations until classifications and deflators are rebuilt.

Method

Conceptual walkthrough of the AI value chain, mapping each stage to existing System of National Accounts rules, satellite accounts, and environmental-economic accounting proposals.

Implications

Policy, firms, and investors need parallel metrics — compute spend, time savings, supply-chain footprints — to steer AI adoption until official statistics absorb the shock.

Measurement playbook

  • Stand up AI satellite accounts for compute, data, and emissions using admin records plus supplier APIs.
  • Instrument quality-adjusted deflators with platform usage logs, health outcomes, or service satisfaction.
  • Adopt MRIO tables and environmental extensions to locate cross-border energy and water footprints.

Risk indicators

  • Labour share erosion concentrated where AI patents cluster; watch bargaining hotspots.
  • Productivity "paradox" likely — falling revenues for automated services even as welfare climbs.
  • Carbon and water misallocation if consumption-based accounts ignore datacentre geography.

Evidence base

  • UK, EU, and OECD experimental statistics show inconsistent AI adoption and energy usage measurement.
  • MRIO datasets (FIGARO, GTAP, EORA) prove feasible templates for tracking cross-border environmental loads.
  • Studies link AI patent density to falling regional labour shares, signalling distributional shifts to monitor.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Forge data-sharing agreements with major AI platforms for anonymised usage and quality metrics.
  • Prototype time-use telemetry panels that plug directly into productivity and welfare dashboards.
  • Harmonise AI input classifications before the 2025 SNA takes effect to avoid a fresh legacy backlog.
  • Bundle AI measurement pilots with policy levers (tax relief, carbon disclosure) to incentivise reporting.
