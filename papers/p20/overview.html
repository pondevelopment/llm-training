The Coasean Singularity? Demand, Supply, and Market Design with AI Agents

Peyman Shahidi • Gili Rusak • Benjamin S. Manning • Andrey Fradkin • John J. Horton • NBER Transformative AI Conference (Sep 2025)

View paper ↗

Shahidi, Rusak, Manning, Fradkin, and Horton map how autonomous AI agents collapse search, negotiation, and monitoring costs—the very frictions Coase said justify firms. Once agents transact, they redraw the boundary between markets and organisations, raising fresh design work for platforms, regulators, and economists.

The chapter walks demand-side adoption (capability, preference discovery, trust), supply-side business models (bring-your-own versus platform "bowling-shoe" agents), and market-level consequences—from congestion and bargaining dynamics to new mechanism design requirements. It also tracks a three-step workflow transition: agents first slot into existing tasks, then reallocate human effort, and finally force end-to-end redesigns once bot-to-bot pipes dominate.

Plain-language explainer

Imagine hiring a digital real-estate broker that never sleeps: it emails every listing, negotiates prices, screens contracts, and even talks to other bots so you only approve the final deal. The authors show how millions of these agents could upend platform design, pricing, job search, and regulation.

Agent workflow evolution

  • Deployment: Agents augment specific steps (search, triage) but humans retain decision rights.
  • Redistribution: As quality climbs, bots take repeatable tasks and people shift to judgement, audits, or higher tempo execution.
  • Reconstruction: Workflows become agent-first (bot-to-bot APIs, automated procurement), with humans setting policy or exceptions.

Executive quick take

Treat agent-to-agent markets as imminent. Governance plans must budget for collapsing transaction costs, platform lock-in wars, and the need for identity plus liability rails.

  • Transaction cost shock: Agents convert human time into compute and APIs, so matching, negotiation, and diligence saturate markets that once rationed exploration.
  • Capability disclosure: Adoption hinges on benchmarks that communicate when agents fail, not on theoretical guarantees.
  • Platform chokepoints: Bring-your-own agents maximise alignment but face throttled access; platform agents embed proprietary data but steer outcomes.
  • Regulatory trilemma: Identity, privacy, and competition goals collide; expect API classes, pay-per-crawl regimes, and agent-only storefronts.

Business relevance

Derivative example: agent procurement exchange

A Fortune 500 supply-chain team deploys a fleet of procurement agents that source bids, negotiate with seller bots, and log carbon disclosures. They run head-to-head tournaments between vendor-provided agents and the firm's own tuned models, with legal watching alignment guardrails before granting wallet access.

  • CPOs: Decide where the marginal negotiation belongs—in-house agents or platform bots—and price compute versus vendor steering.
  • CISOs: Enforce agent identity, sandboxing, and prompt logging so delegated spending stays auditable.
  • Product leads: Build agent-first APIs with rate limits, fee schedules, and preferencing policies before third-party bots swamp human UIs.
  • Legal & policy: Stress-test liability when your bot signs contracts or misprices bids; update indemnities and insurance.

Demand triggers

Agents appear first where intermediation is already costly.

  • High stakes: Jobs, real estate, and finance transactions justify compute-intensive diligence.
  • Large search spaces: Dating, freelancing, and rentals reward agents that evaluate thousands of options.
  • High evaluation effort: Procurement, funding rounds, and college admissions favour agents that can grind through every document and metric.
  • Information asymmetry: Used assets and insurance benefit when bots surface hidden quality signals.
  • Experience gaps: Infrequent buyers (first-home purchase, estate planning) can rent the wisdom of frequent transactors.
  • Preference discovery: Agents elicit hazy tastes (job-fit, housing amenities) and learn from natural-language briefs when humans cannot rank everything explicitly.

Supply archetypes

Shahidi et al. outline a 2x2 of ownership versus specialisation.

  • Bring-your-own horizontal: User-controlled agent roaming multiple markets; privacy benefits but limited privileged hooks.
  • Bring-your-own vertical: Specialist third-party agent with deep domain tooling yet still portable across platforms in that niche.
  • Platform horizontal: Default OS or marketplace bot with first-party telemetry and steering incentives.
  • Platform vertical: Deeply integrated provider bot that optimises conversions but cements lock-in.

Pricing dynamics

  • Compute-metered tiers: High-stakes deals may rent extra compute cycles, raising price discrimination questions.
  • Freemium & ad-supported agents: Platforms can subsidise onboarding, monetising ancillary services or data.
  • Versioned access: Expect throttled free bots, paid pro agents, and transaction-fee hybrids as markets settle.
  • Cross-platform subsidies: Hardware, logistics, or payments arms can bundle agents, shifting competition policy scrutiny.

Designing aligned agents

  • Preference elicitation: Agents translate messy natural-language briefs into ranked options but still inherit the principal's articulation limits.
  • Preference discovery: Long-run observation lets bots surface latent tastes (sunlight, commute tolerance) and prompt users to confirm them.
  • Alignment safeguards: Providers must guard against hallucination and mis-specified objectives while keeping contracts legible to non-technical buyers.
  • Adoption boundaries: Markets with manageable preference dimensionality will adopt faster; complex taste vectors still strain agent synthesis.
  • Evaluation flywheel: Benchmarks, word-of-mouth, and even agent-on-agent interviews help principals vet capability, with bootstrapped testing becoming a standard onboarding step.
Key insight

When agents make search and bargaining effectively free, platform rules, identity rails, and congestion management replace human time as the binding factors in market efficiency.

Method

Conceptual synthesis mixing transaction-cost economics, platform design archetypes, emerging agent deployments, and regulatory case studies (Cloudflare pay-per-crawl, NYC AEDT, EU liability).

Implications

Prepare markets for agent-first APIs, pricing that reflects compute usage, and governance that separates legitimate automation from sybil spam, data leakage, and self-preferencing.

Market design checklist

  • Ship agent-specific surfaces: authenticated APIs, explicit fee tables, and policy-aware throttles.
  • Expose consent and robots rules that bots must read before crawling; meter heavy usage with pay-per-crawl or compute tolls.
  • Adopt matching mechanisms (deferred acceptance, auctions) once agents can supply full preference orderings.
  • Benchmark agent failure modes alongside human baselines so principals know when to intervene.
  • Rework monetisation toward subscription or API access when agent filters erode ad-driven playbooks.

Risk signals

  • Application congestion: AI-written resumes or purchase bids flood platforms, degrading selection quality.
  • Bargaining reshaped: Bots negotiate earlier, longer, and cheaper, pressuring human-led pricing structures.
  • Identity spoofing: Sybil attacks grow easier unless proof-of-personhood or credential layers accompany agents.
  • Data leakage & IP drift: Agents scrape content and cross-context data unless provenance and usage rights are enforced.

Evidence base

  • Early deployments: Google's Project Mariner and Walmart's shopping agent prototypes already transact on behalf of users.
  • Policy moves: Cloudflare's pay-per-crawl API and NYC's Automated Employment Decision Tool law show how infrastructure and oversight are adapting.
  • Research traction: Agent-to-agent negotiation experiments and preference-transfer studies (Rusak et al., 2025) validate the behavioural assumptions.
  • Privacy & IP rulings: EU product liability updates and newsroom-vs-model lawsuits underline the need for provenance controls in agent ecosystems.

Regulatory watch

  • Concentration risks: antitrust cases and interoperability mandates aim to keep compute, data, and distribution open to challengers.
  • Liability allocation: EU-style product directives extend responsibility to software updates, while jailbreak and misuse scenarios strain negligence tests.
  • Privacy guardrails: expect refreshed consent law, provenance tags, and opt-out registries as agents remix sensitive data across contexts.
  • Creator compensation: collective licensing, API tolls, and tamper-evident metadata balance agent crawling with IP rights.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Instrument identity: combine proof-of-personhood with verifiable credentials so humans can delegate safely.
  • Design price levers: experiment with compute-based fees, agent licensing, or congestion pricing before bots overwhelm channels.
  • Stage liability pilots: clarify when harm flows to principals, developers, or platforms as agents sign contracts or misbehave.
  • Stand up agent sandboxes: regulators and platforms should co-test agent APIs, benchmarks, and kill-switch procedures.
  • Protect data rights: embed opt-out registries, licensing frameworks, and audit trails to manage training leakage and privacy claims.
