About the experiment

Participants solved 7 Cognitive Reflection Test items with or without an AI assistant. The AI was secretly set to give correct answers on some trials and confidently wrong answers on others. Participants could freely use or ignore the AI.

How to read this

Step 1 ‚Äî The setup: Researchers secretly controlled the AI. In some trials it gave the correct answer; in others it confidently gave a wrong answer. Participants didn't know which was which.

Step 2 ‚Äî Human accuracy: The bars below show how often humans got the right answer in each condition. If people think for themselves, accuracy should stay near Brain-Only even when AI is wrong. If they surrender to AI, accuracy soars with correct AI but collapses with faulty AI.

The surrender gap: We measure surrender by comparing AI-Faulty accuracy against Brain-Only. If people think independently, AI-Faulty should stay near Brain-Only ‚Äî wrong AI shouldn't hurt you if you ignore it. But if AI-Faulty drops below Brain-Only, it means the wrong AI actively made people worse ‚Äî they replaced their own thinking with whatever the AI said. That's cognitive surrender.

Key fact: Of those who consulted the AI when it was wrong, ‚Äî adopted its incorrect answer. Combined with brain-only accuracy already being low, this crushed overall accuracy on faulty-AI trials ‚Äî see AI-Faulty below.
