Executive quick take

Kerr’s classic essay catalogs how teams celebrate metrics that are easy to count while quietly hoping for harder-to-measure behaviors. Unless rewards and reviews match the real mission, organizations reliably get more of the wrong behavior—no matter how inspiring the strategy slide looks.

  • People optimise for the incentives that actually trigger bonuses, promotions, or public praise.
  • Misaligned rewards compound over time, creating norms that crowd out intrinsic motivation.
  • Fixes start with making implicit trade-offs explicit, then redesigning rewards to buy the outcomes you want.

Business relevance

Treat Kerr’s framework as a diagnostic when OKR or KPI programs drift. It reveals when dashboards, quarterly targets, or performance reviews reward busywork while leaders “hope” for long-term health, quality, or ethics.

  • Revenue leaders: Balance quota clocks with relationship depth so reps don’t torch renewals.
  • Product & R&D: Tie career growth to validated learning, not just feature velocity.
  • Risk & compliance: Reward issues surfaced, not merely issues avoided, or they will stay hidden.
  • People teams: Align recognition programs with collaborative outcomes, not heroic individual fire drills.

Derivative example: Incentive alignment review

Run a quarterly workshop where each VP lists their top three desired behaviors and the actual rewards attached (bonuses, dashboards, shout-outs). Map mismatches on a heatmap, assign owners to redesign the reward, and track leading indicators (quality audits, renewal uplift, cycle times) after the change.

Why misaligned incentives persist

  • Organizations default to measuring what is easy (counts, deadlines) instead of what is meaningful.
  • Legacy reward systems survive leadership changes because no one owns sunsetting them.
  • Cognitive dissonance: teams convince themselves that symbolic rewards will somehow produce stretch goals.

How to run the audit

  • List the outcomes you truly need (B) and gather evidence of what actually gets rewarded (A).
  • Score each reward on immediacy, visibility, and payout magnitude to see which behaviors it amplifies.
  • Prototype new incentives with small cohorts; verify they change behavior before scaling firm-wide.
Key insight

Rewards that contradict stated goals do more damage than no reward at all—they legitimise counter-productive habits while shielding leaders from the truth.

Method

Kerr synthesises field observations across universities, military units, corporations, and governments to show the pattern repeats regardless of sector or seniority.

Implication

Leaders must design incentive systems deliberately—even anecdotally—because employees will assume incentives express the real strategy, not the town-hall slogans.

Evidence

  • Universities proclaim teaching excellence yet base tenure decisions on publications rather than classroom impact.
  • Hospitals hope physicians prevent illness, but fee-for-service rewards procedures and short-term throughput.
  • Military campaigns emphasized enemy body counts, encouraging tactics that undermined long-term pacification.
  • Corporations praise long-range health while incentive plans fixate on quarterly earnings and budget adherence.

Forward-looking roadmap

  • Maintain an incentive register that links every reward to the behaviors and metrics it is meant to reinforce.
  • Pair financial rewards with recognition rituals so the desired behaviors stay visible after the payout.
  • Introduce “failure bonuses” for surfaced risks or experiment shutdowns to keep honesty and learning in play.
  • Audit incentives quarterly as part of risk reviews, not just during annual compensation cycles.
