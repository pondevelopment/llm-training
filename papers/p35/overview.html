This study provides the first large-scale empirical evidence on GenAI's impact on scientific productivity. Using matched author-level panel data from 32,480 social science researchers (2021-2024), the authors find that GenAI adoption increased publication output by 36% in 2024 compared to non-users, with modest gains in journal quality (+2%). Effects are strongest among early-career researchers, technically complex fields, and non-English-speaking countries.

Why keyword detection works for adoption tracking

GenAI models (especially GPT-3.5/4) exhibit distinctive lexical patternsâ€”words like "delve," "meticulous," "intricate," and "underscore" appear at higher frequencies in LLM-generated text than human writing. The study tracks 65 such markers, filtered to those showing â‰¥200% frequency increases between 2022 and 2024. This method is computationally lightweight, transparent, and replicable across large datasets. While it doesn't capture GenAI use for coding or data analysis, it reliably identifies text-based adoptionâ€”the primary bottleneck in academic publishing.

Propensity score matching: isolating the GenAI effect

Self-selection bias is a major threat: perhaps GenAI adopters are simply more productive, tech-savvy, or ambitious. The authors address this with nearest-neighbor propensity score matching (3:1 control ratio), balancing pre-treatment publication counts, impact factors, career age, gender, field, and language proximity to English. Combined with author fixed effects, this design controls for time-invariant differences and ensures comparisons track within-person changes over time, not cross-sectional differences between high and low performers.

Method

Difference-in-differences with propensity score matching on 32,480 social science authors (2021-2024), identifying GenAI users via lexical markers in titles/abstracts, controlling for career stage, field, gender, and language background.

Implication

Institutions delaying GenAI access may inadvertently widen productivity gaps. Early evidence suggests targeted subsidies for non-English speakers and junior researchers yield highest ROI.
