Key insight

AI chatbot adoption has been worker-driven and rapid (47% baseline, 83% with encouragement), but labor market rigidities, weak bargaining power for bottom-up tool adoption, and limited workflow integration create a massive gap between tool usage and economic outcomes.

Method

Two-wave survey (Nov 2023, Nov 2024) of 25,000 workers at 7,000 workplaces across 11 AI-exposed occupations in Denmark, linked to monthly administrative earnings/hours data. Difference-in-differences comparing adopters vs. non-adopters before/after ChatGPT launch (Nov 2022), leveraging employer policies as quasi-experimental variation.

Implication

GenAI's labor market impact is occurring on a much longer timescale than technology adoption curves suggest. Organizations investing in chatbots should expect years-long productivity J-curves, not quarters. Measure actual output changes (deals closed, tickets resolved, features shipped) rather than adoption rates or sentiment.

Understanding the RCT vs. field gap

Randomized controlled trials consistently show 15â€“50% productivity gains from AI chatbots (e.g., Noy & Zhang: 37% for writing tasks; Brynjolfsson et al.: 15% for customer support; Peng et al.: 58% for coding). This study finds only 2.8% real-world time savings. Three factors explain the gap:

  • Limited applicability: Users apply chatbots every 3rd-4th day and spend only 5â€“6% of work hours actively using them, even on usage days
  • Occupational heterogeneity: RCTs focus on high-impact tasks (support tickets, copywriting, code completion); broader occupations (teachers, accountants, financial advisors) see half the gains
  • Lack of complementary investment: RCTs provide ideal conditions (workflow integration, clear use cases, training); real workers rarely have these supports unless employers actively encourage use

Why weak pass-through matters

In competitive labor markets, workers' productivity gains should pass through 1:1 to wages (Becker 1964). This study finds only 3â€“7% pass-through, closer to firm-level productivity shocks (Card et al. 2018: 5â€“15%) than worker-specific skill gains (Balke & Lamadon 2022: 26%). Three mechanisms:

  • Principal-agent frictions: Bottom-up adoption without managerial buy-in weakens workers' ability to claim credit or negotiate raises based on chatbot-assisted output
  • Task vs. output gains: Saving time on existing tasks doesn't increase marginal productivity unless workflows adapt to assign more valuable workâ€”which rarely happens without employer coordination
  • Monopsony power: Workers face search frictions and switching costs; employers capture most of the surplus from productivity improvements, especially for general-purpose tools workers can't easily credential
