About this framework

This research agenda emerged from recognizing that AI technologies differ fundamentally from prior digital technologies in three ways: (1) capacity to constrain, complement, or substitute for humans, shifting locus of control away from exclusive human domain; (2) challenging divisions between human ability and machine capability by performing feats like emotion recognition, creativity, and autonomous decision-making; (3) exhibiting complexity that creates dual outcomes requiring multi-stakeholder perspectives.

The framework organizes research opportunities around four business capabilities rather than technology types (e.g., "neural networks" or "chatbots") because capabilities better capture organizational impact. Each capability invokes specific tensions that require distinct research approaches and governance strategies. Organizations deploying AI across multiple capabilities face coordination challenges and need integrated governance frameworks.

Methodological note: The paper synthesizes existing IS literature on AI with insights from organizational theory, ethics, and human-machine interaction to identify research gaps. Each tension represents areas where traditional IS theories (adoption, implementation, use, control) require extension or reconceptualization to account for AI's distinctive characteristics.
