Can AI act as a genuine teammate rather than just a tool? Dell'Acqua, Ayoubi, Lifshitz, Sadun, and colleagues conducted a randomized field experiment with 776 Procter & Gamble professionals to find out. Participants worked on real product innovation challenges in four conditions: individuals without AI, two-person cross-functional teams without AI, individuals with AI, and teams with AI. The results challenge conventional wisdom: individuals working with AI matched the performance of two-person teams working without AI. Even more striking, AI broke down functional silosâ€”R&D professionals produced commercially viable ideas and Commercial professionals generated technically sound solutions when using AI, regardless of their background. Finally, AI users reported significantly more positive emotions (excitement, energy, enthusiasm) and less negative emotions (anxiety, frustration), suggesting AI fulfilled some of the motivational benefits traditionally provided by human teammates.

Executive Quick Take: The Practical Ceiling

The finding: AI doesn't just augment individual workersâ€”it can substitute for certain collaborative functions. Individuals with AI performed at the same level as traditional two-person cross-functional teams (0.37 vs. 0.24 standard deviation improvement over solo baseline), completed work 16.4% faster, and produced longer, more comprehensive solutions. AI also democratized expertise: less-experienced workers with AI matched teams containing experienced colleagues, and professionals crossed functional boundaries they typically couldn't span alone.

The nuance: While AI-enabled individuals matched team performance on average, AI-augmented teams were more likely to produce top-tier solutions (ranking in top 10% of all submissions). This suggests AI works both as a substitute for collaboration (individuals can work alone effectively) and as a complement to teamwork (teams with AI produce breakthrough results more often). Organizations focused on efficiency may shift toward smaller teams, while those prioritizing exceptional innovation may double down on AI-augmented collaboration.

When to use AI as a teammate substitute: Early-stage ideation, time-sensitive deliverables, knowledge work with clear evaluation criteria, situations where coordination costs are high, and tasks requiring boundary-spanning expertise that would normally need multiple specialists.

When to keep human teams (with AI): High-stakes innovation where top 10% outcomes matter disproportionately, complex problems requiring iterative refinement, contexts where team learning and skill development are strategic priorities, and situations where relationship-building has independent value.

Business Relevance: Rethinking Team Design

For team leads and project managers: The traditional assumptionâ€”that cross-functional problems require cross-functional teamsâ€”no longer holds universally. An individual with AI can now produce balanced, comprehensive solutions that previously required assembling representatives from multiple functions. This has immediate implications for sprint planning, task allocation, and coordination overhead. Consider allowing individuals to tackle problems solo with AI support before escalating to team formation, especially for early-stage ideation or when scheduling conflicts create delays.

For HR and organizational design: Optimal team size may be smaller than previously thought, particularly for knowledge work in the "fuzzy front end" of innovation. However, don't eliminate teams entirelyâ€”the data shows AI-augmented teams produce exceptional results more frequently. The question shifts from "team or no team?" to "what team size and composition maximize the probability of breakthrough outcomes for this specific challenge?" For learning and development: Two critical skill areas emerge: (1) AI interaction proficiencyâ€”learning to prompt, validate, and integrate AI outputs effectively (participants in this study were relatively inexperienced, suggesting these benefits may represent a lower bound), and (2) boundary-spanning thinkingâ€”using AI to reason across traditional domain boundaries rather than staying in functional lanes. For innovation pipeline owners: Higher-quality "seeds" at the front end of the innovation funnel translate to better outcomes downstream. AI enables both speed (16.4% time savings) and comprehensiveness (significantly longer, more detailed solutions). This creates opportunities to either increase throughput (more ideas evaluated in the same time) or deepen evaluation (more thorough exploration of fewer concepts). The increased likelihood of top-tier outcomes from AI-augmented teams suggests concentrating resources on smaller, AI-enabled expert teams for the most strategic challenges.

Evidence: What the Study Found

Experimental Design

Between May and July 2024, researchers randomized 776 P&G professionals (from Commercial and R&D functions across Baby Care, Feminine Care, Grooming, and Oral Care business units in Europe and Americas) to four conditions in a one-day virtual product development workshop:

  • Control: Individual without AI
  • Treatment 1: Two-person team (1 Commercial + 1 R&D professional) without AI
  • Treatment 2: Individual with AI (GPT-4 via Microsoft Azure)
  • Treatment 3: Two-person team with AI

Participants tackled real business challenges from their own unitsâ€”developing ideas for new products, packaging, communication approaches, or retail executionâ€”using P&G's standard processes and data. AI-enabled participants received one-hour training on prompting techniques for consumer goods tasks. Teams collaborated via Microsoft Teams (standard P&G practice), with one member randomly designated to share screen and submit. Solutions were evaluated by blind human experts on quality (1â€“10 scale), novelty, and feasibility. Participants also reported emotions before and after the task.

Performance: AI Enables Individuals to Match Teams

Quality improvements over baseline (individuals without AI):

  • Teams without AI: +0.24 standard deviations (p < 0.05) â€” traditional collaboration benefit
  • Individuals with AI: +0.37 standard deviations (p < 0.01) â€” matching team performance
  • Teams with AI: +0.39 standard deviations (p < 0.01) â€” not significantly different from individuals with AI

The performance hierarchy is clear: solo work without AI produced the lowest quality; adding either a human teammate or AI assistant produced similar gains; combining both (team + AI) did not significantly improve average quality beyond individual + AI. However, when focusing on top 10% solutions (the breakthrough ideas that drive outsized returns), teams with AI were 9.2 percentage points more likely to produce top-tier work (roughly 3Ã— the baseline rate of 5.8%), while individuals with AI showed no significant increase in top-tier probability.

Efficiency gains: AI also accelerated work. Individuals with AI completed tasks 16.4% faster than solo baseline; teams with AI saved 12.7% time. At the same time, AI users produced significantly longer solutions (more comprehensive, detailed proposals) despite working fasterâ€”a double benefit of speed and thoroughness.

Expertise: Breaking Down Functional Silos

Democratizing task expertise: The study split participants by familiarity with product development tasksâ€”"core-job" employees (regularly do this work) versus "non-core-job" employees (less familiar). Without AI, non-core-job individuals performed poorly; even in teams, their performance barely improved. With AI, however, non-core-job individuals working alone matched the performance of teams containing core-job experts. AI effectively substituted for the expertise and guidance typically provided by experienced teammates, democratizing access to domain knowledge.

Crossing functional boundaries: The most striking finding concerns professional silos. Without AI, Commercial professionals proposed predominantly commercial solutions (market-focused, less technical) while R&D professionals favored technical approaches (engineering-heavy, less commercial). When teams worked without AI, they produced more balanced solutions through cross-functional collaborationâ€”the traditional benefit of diverse teams. Remarkably, when individuals used AI, this functional distinction disappeared. Both Commercial and R&D professionals generated solutions with similar levels of technical and commercial balance, effectively replicating the boundary-spanning knowledge integration of human teams. Quality scores did not vary by solution technicality, confirming these effects didn't sacrifice effectiveness for breadth.

AI also reduced "dominance effects" in teams. Teams without AI showed bimodal solution distributions (clustering around either technical or commercial poles, depending on which member dominated). Teams with AI produced more uniform, balanced distributions while maintaining similar technical rangeâ€”suggesting AI helped ensure both perspectives contributed more equally.

Sociality: Positive Emotional Responses

Contrary to fears that AI creates negative workplace experiences, participants using AI reported consistently positive emotional responses:

  • Individuals with AI: +0.457 standard deviations in positive emotions (excitement, energy, enthusiasm) (p < 0.01)
  • Teams with AI: +0.635 standard deviations in positive emotions (p < 0.01)
  • Both individuals and teams with AI reported significant decreases in negative emotions (anxiety, frustration): -0.233 and -0.235 standard deviations respectively (p < 0.05)

Notably, individuals using AI reported positive emotional responses matching or exceeding those of team members working without AI, suggesting AI can substitute for some of the motivational and social benefits traditionally associated with human collaboration. Participants who reported larger increases in expected future AI use also showed more positive emotions, indicating positive experiences may drive adoption.

Surprising Gap: Actual vs. Perceived Performance

Despite objective quality improvements, AI users showed lower confidence in their own work. Participants using AI were 9.2 percentage points less likely to expect their solutions to rank in the top 10% (p < 0.05) compared to controls, even though their actual performance was higher. This confidence gap suggests workers may not yet recognize the value AI adds to their outputâ€”an important consideration for adoption and change management.

AI Usage Patterns

Analysis of AI interaction logs revealed diverse usage patterns. Many participants showed high "retention rates" (>75% of final solution text originated from AI), but this didn't indicate passive copyingâ€”participants with 100% AI-originated content averaged 23.9 prompts, suggesting extensive iterative refinement. Conversely, some participants showed zero retention, using AI purely for ideation and brainstorming rather than direct content generation. Semantic similarity analysis confirmed that while AI-aided solutions were more consistent with each other than human-only solutions, they remained much closer to human patterns than to naive AI outputs, indicating humans meaningfully shaped and contextualized AI suggestions.

Roadmap: Strategic Implications

Immediate Actions (0â€“6 Months)

  • Pilot AI-enabled individual workflows for early-stage innovation â€” Test whether solo workers with AI can effectively handle ideation and concept development tasks that currently require convening cross-functional teams.   • Adjust performance expectations and confidence calibration â€” Educate teams that AI users may underestimate their own performance quality. Build feedback loops that show workers their actual outcomes compared to peers, helping them recognize AI's contribution.
  • Experiment with smaller, AI-augmented teams for breakthrough innovation â€” For high-stakes challenges where top 10% outcomes matter, form small expert teams with AI support rather than larger human-only groups.

Medium-Term Initiatives (6â€“18 Months)

  • Redesign work allocation and team formation policies â€” Create explicit decision frameworks: When should we assign one person + AI? When should we form a small team + AI?   • Develop AI-enabled collaborative systems â€” Current AI tools weren't optimized for teamwork.   • Build organizational learning systems for AI interaction patterns â€” Capture and disseminate best practices from high-performing AI users. Identify which prompting strategies, validation approaches, and integration techniques produce the best outcomes, and scale them across teams.
  • Measure expertise development effects longitudinally â€” Does sustained AI-enabled boundary-spanning lead to genuine skill development, or does it only facilitate access to existing knowledge? Long-Term Considerations (18+ Months)

  • Rethink organizational structure and optimal team size â€” If individuals with AI can consistently replicate team performance, the fundamental assumptions underlying organizational design shift.   • Balance efficiency gains with learning and development â€” While AI enables smaller teams and faster work, traditional teamwork also serves learning, mentorship, and culture-building functions.   • Investigate how these patterns evolve with AI proficiency â€” Study participants were relatively inexperienced with AI, suggesting observed benefits may be a lower bound.   • Explore implications for remote vs. co-located work â€” If AI substitutes for some collaborative functions, the traditional benefits of physical proximity may diminish further. This could accelerate flexible work arrangements, but organizations must consciously design for the remaining benefits of in-person interaction (trust-building, serendipitous knowledge transfer, culture reinforcement).

Open Questions for Future Research
