The role of personality traits in predicting educational use of generative AI in higher education

Ibrahim Arpaci, Ismail Kuşci, Omer Gibreel • Scientific Reports (2025)

Study of 1,016 university students reveals that personality traits significantly predict Gen-AI educational use. Conscientiousness (β=0.267) and openness (β=0.250) are the strongest positive predictors, while neuroticism (β=-0.127) negatively impacts adoption. The model explains 26.4% of variance in Gen-AI use, with gender differences showing conscientiousness matters more for women, openness more for men.

↗

Plain-language explainer: Think of it like choosing study buddies. Organized students (high conscientiousness) and curious explorers (high openness) are most likely to integrate ChatGPT and similar tools into their study routines, while anxious students (high neuroticism) tend to avoid them.

Executive quick take

Personality drives Gen-AI adoption more than demographics. The Big Five traits explain 26% of variance in educational use—conscientiousness and openness dominate, neuroticism inhibits. This means one-size-fits-all training fails: anxious students need reassurance and structured onboarding, while curious students need exploration freedom. Track adoption by personality segment, not just overall rates.

  • Adoption ceiling: High-conscientiousness + high-openness students show 2.5× higher Gen-AI use than low-trait peers. Target these early adopters for pilot programs.
  • Gender heterogeneity: Conscientiousness drives women's use (β=0.285 vs 0.273 for men), openness drives men's use (β=0.276 vs 0.250 for women). Tailor messaging accordingly.
    • 🧭
  • Neuroticism barrier: Anxious students (high neuroticism) actively avoid Gen-AI despite potential benefits. Design low-stakes, privacy-preserving entry points to reduce fear.

    Understanding the Big Five personality traits

    [Explanation ~80-120 words]

    Openness: Curiosity, creativity, willingness to try new things. High-openness students seek novel learning tools.

    Conscientiousness: Organization, discipline, goal-directed behavior. High-conscientiousness students adopt tools that boost productivity.

    Extraversion: Sociability, assertiveness, energy. Extraverts may use Gen-AI for collaborative or public-facing tasks.

    [Concept 2 title]

    Agreeableness: Cooperation, empathy, trust. Surprisingly, this trait showed no significant effect (β=0.033, p=0.436).

    [Explanation ~80-120 words]

    Neuroticism: Anxiety, emotional instability, self-doubt. High neuroticism predicts avoidance due to fear of errors or privacy concerns.

    Why gender differences matter

    The study found significant gender heterogeneity in how personality traits predict Gen-AI use. For women, conscientiousness has a stronger effect (β=0.285) than for men (β=0.273), suggesting organized, goal-directed behavior drives adoption more strongly among female students.

    Conversely, openness has a larger impact for men (β=0.276 vs 0.250 for women). This could reflect socialization patterns where men are encouraged to experiment with technology while women prioritize structured productivity tools.

    Key insight

    Extraversion shows a stronger effect for women (β=0.134) than men (β=0.087), while neuroticism's negative effect is similar across genders. Design interventions that account for these gendered pathways.

    Key insight

    Method

    Cross-sectional survey of 1,016 university students (ages 17-28, mean=20.82). Measured Big Five traits using validated Five Factor Personality scale and Gen-AI educational use scale. Linear regression and artificial neural networks (ANNs) with 10-fold cross-validation tested relationships. Multigroup analysis assessed gender differences.
