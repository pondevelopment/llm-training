Understanding the Big Five personality traits

Openness: Curiosity, creativity, willingness to try new things. High-openness students seek novel learning tools.

Conscientiousness: Organization, discipline, goal-directed behavior. High-conscientiousness students adopt tools that boost productivity.

Extraversion: Sociability, assertiveness, energy. Extraverts may use Gen-AI for collaborative or public-facing tasks.

Agreeableness: Cooperation, empathy, trust. Surprisingly, this trait showed no significant effect (Î²=0.033, p=0.436).

Neuroticism: Anxiety, emotional instability, self-doubt. High neuroticism predicts avoidance due to fear of errors or privacy concerns.

Why gender differences matter

The study found significant gender heterogeneity in how personality traits predict Gen-AI use. For women, conscientiousness has a stronger effect (Î²=0.285) than for men (Î²=0.273), suggesting organized, goal-directed behavior drives adoption more strongly among female students.

Conversely, openness has a larger impact for men (Î²=0.276 vs 0.250 for women). This could reflect socialization patterns where men are encouraged to experiment with technology while women prioritize structured productivity tools.

Extraversion shows a stronger effect for women (Î²=0.134) than men (Î²=0.087), while neuroticism's negative effect is similar across genders. Design interventions that account for these gendered pathways.

Method

Cross-sectional survey of 1,016 university students (ages 17-28, mean=20.82). Measured Big Five traits using validated Five Factor Personality scale and Gen-AI educational use scale. Linear regression and artificial neural networks (ANNs) with 10-fold cross-validation tested relationships. Multigroup analysis assessed gender differences.

Implication

Universal Gen-AI training programs will fail to reach 40-50% of students. Instead, use personality-based segmentation to deliver tailored onboarding: exploratory for high-openness, productivity-focused for high-conscientiousness, reassuring for high-neuroticism. Track adoption metrics by segment to identify where interventions are needed.

For your roadmap

If you're deploying Gen-AI tools in education or corporate training, personality-blind strategies will leave engagement on the table. Here's how to operationalize these findings:

  • Pre-assess personality traits: Use short Big Five inventory (10-50 items) during onboarding. Store trait profiles to customize experiences without invasive tracking.
  • Design three adoption tracks: Explorer (high openness: creative prompts, experimentation), Achiever (high conscientiousness: productivity workflows, structured outputs), Cautious (high neuroticism: privacy controls, validation tools, gradual onboarding).
  • Monitor adoption by segment: Break Gen-AI usage metrics by personality profile. If high-openness users show low adoption, your tool may be too rigid. If high-neuroticism users avoid it, trust/safety messaging needs work.
  • Address gender differences: Test whether conscientiousness-focused messaging resonates more with women, openness-focused with men. Avoid stereotyping but use data to optimize outreach.
  • Run longitudinal cohort study: Track personality traits, Gen-AI use, and learning outcomes over 1-2 semesters. Measure whether personality-aware interventions close adoption gaps and improve performance for previously-excluded segments.
